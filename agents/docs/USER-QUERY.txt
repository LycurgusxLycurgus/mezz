# English Learning Protocol for Spanish Speakers

This document is a **complete manual** for learning **English** when your native language is **Spanish**.

It is **self‑referential**: it explains the method, the words it uses, the tests, and how to use the document itself.

If you feel lost, do this:

1. Go to **Section 2 (Roadmap)**.
2. Pick your **current step**.
3. Follow the checklist for that step.

---

## 1) The idea in one sentence

You learn English fast when you do this in order:
**Blueprint → Sound → Core words → Real input/output → High‑stakes practice → Repeat**.

---

## 2) Roadmap (the 4 Modules)

These 4 modules repeat forever. Each cycle is harder.

* **Module I — Deconstruction (Legos):** find the basic grammar rules by using a few “golden sentences.”
* **Module II — Selection (80/20):** learn the small set of words and patterns that appear all the time.
* **Module III — Sequencing (Real Life):** use English with easy real content (input) and simple speaking (output).
* **Module IV — Stakes (High Stakes):** do English in your specific domain (work, study, travel, crypto, etc.) with pressure.

**Rule:** you do not “finish” English. You do cycles.

Before you start a cycle, answer this:

* What is my goal this cycle? (Example: “I want to hold a 10‑minute call about my job.”)

---

## 3) What “mastery” means here

Mastery is not “I know many words.” Mastery is:

* **Understanding:** you can explain the rule in simple words.
* **Memorization:** you can recall the word/pattern later.
* **Prediction:** you can guess what comes next in a sentence or conversation.
* **Creation:** you can build new sentences for new situations.

If you only memorize, you are fragile. If you can predict and create, you are strong.

---

## 4) Plain words (mini glossary)

This document uses a few terms. Here they are in simple language.

* **Blueprint:** a small map of how English works, before you learn many words.
* **Golden sentences:** a tiny set of sentences that show many core grammar rules.
* **Shadowing:** you listen, then you copy the sound immediately (like a parrot, but careful).
* **IPA:** a “sound alphabet.” It tells you the real sound, not the confusing spelling.
* **SRS (Spaced Repetition):** a memory system that shows you a card **right before you forget**.
* **Comprehensible input:** content you mostly understand (about 70–80%). A little hard, not too hard.
* **Language island:** a short speech you memorize perfectly (a safe “island” to start conversations).
* **Gate:** a rule to move forward. You must pass the gate.

---

## 5) Why this method works (the principles)

### Principle A — Map first, details later

If you learn random words first, you feel busy but you are blind.
A blueprint stops confusion. You see the “machine”.

**Metaphor:**

* Grammar is the **skeleton**.
* Words are the **meat**.
  You need both. But the skeleton gives structure.

### Principle B — Sound first, spelling later

English spelling is tricky. If you read too early, you may lock a strong Spanish accent.
You want your brain to store **sound → meaning**.

**Metaphor:**
Words are like songs. If you learn the lyrics without the melody, you sing wrong.

### Principle C — Small set, used a lot

The most common words build most sentences.
Learn the “busy” words first.

### Principle D — Memory needs timing

If you don’t review, you forget.
SRS is a “forgetting alarm.”

### Principle E — Input builds intuition

When you see patterns many times, your brain learns rules without lectures.
Babies do this. Adults can do it too.

### Principle F — Output makes you honest

Speaking and writing show your real level.
Mistakes are not shame. Mistakes are data.

---

## 6) The method (step‑by‑step)

This is the full algorithm. Each step has:

* **Goal** (what you are building)
* **Daily actions** (small repeatable work)
* **Gate** (how you prove you can move on)

### Step 0 — Setup (1 day)

**Goal:** create a simple learning machine.

**Actions:**

* Choose your **English variant**: General American or British (pick one for consistency).
* Pick your **SRS tool** (Anki or similar).
* Pick 1–2 sources for easy input (graded reader, beginner podcast, simple videos).

**Gate:** you can describe your plan in 3 simple sentences.

---

### Step 1 — Blueprint (Days 1–2) — “Golden Sentences”

**Goal:** see the core grammar quickly.

**Actions:**

1. Get these Spanish sentences translated into English by a native speaker or AI.
2. Put them in a small table: Spanish → English.
3. Analyze the patterns (not memorization yet).

#### The 12 Golden Sentences (Spanish → English)

1. **La manzana es roja.** → The apple is red.
2. **Es la manzana de Juan.** → It is John’s apple.
3. **Le doy la manzana a Juan.** → I give John the apple.
4. **Se la damos.** → We give it to him.
5. **Él se la da a Juan.** → He gives it to John.
6. **Ella se la da a él.** → She gives it to him.
7. **Debo dársela.** → I must give it to him.
8. **Quiero dársela.** → I want to give it to her.
9. **Voy a saber mañana.** → I’m going to know tomorrow.
10. **No puedo comer la manzana.** → I cannot / can’t eat the apple.
11. **Me he comido la manzana.** → I have eaten the apple.
12. **Me como la manzana.** → I eat the apple.

#### How to analyze (ask these questions)

Look at the English lines and ask:

* Where is the adjective? (red apple / apple red)
* Does English use **articles** (a/an/the) where Spanish may omit?
* What changes when the subject changes? (I give / he gives)
* How does English show **possession**? (John’s)
* Where does **not** go? (can’t eat)
* How does English build questions? (later you will add: Do you…?)
* Where does time live? (tomorrow)

#### Quick English facts you should notice

* English word order is often **Subject–Verb–Object**.
* In simple present, **he/she/it** often adds **-s** (he gives).
* Negation often needs an **auxiliary** (can’t, don’t, doesn’t).
* English uses **articles** a lot (a/an/the).

**Gate (Blueprint):**

* You can explain, in Spanish or simple English, 5 rules you discovered from the sentences.
* You can produce 10 new sentences by swapping words (apple → book; John → Maria) while keeping the same pattern.

---

### Step 2 — Sound Lab (Week 1) — “Phonetic Upload”

**Goal:** train ears + mouth so your English is clear.

**Rule:** for the first week, **limit reading**. Use audio first.

**Daily actions (20–40 min):**

1. **Listen** to clear slow English (beginner audio).
2. **Shadow**: repeat immediately, copy rhythm and stress.
3. Record yourself once per day (30 seconds).
4. Fix only **one** sound per day.

#### The big difference: Spanish vs English rhythm

* Spanish is more **syllable‑timed** (each syllable similar length).
* English is more **stress‑timed** (some syllables are strong, many are weak).

**Metaphor:** English is like a drumbeat: **STRONG weak weak STRONG weak**.

#### Common Spanish‑speaker sound traps (English)

Work on these early:

* **/ɪ/ vs /iː/**: ship vs sheep
* **/æ/**: cat (not “ket”)
* **/ʌ/**: cup (not “cap”)
* **TH**: **/θ/** (think) and **/ð/** (this)
* **H**: house (don’t drop the h)
* Final consonants: “work”, “tests” (don’t eat the last sound)
* **-ed endings**: worked /t/, played /d/, wanted /ɪd/

If you want to use IPA, keep it small. Learn only the sounds you need.

**Gate (Sound):**

* You can read your 12 sentences out loud with clear stress and no “Spanish reading voice.”
* A listener can understand you without asking “What?” more than once per minute.

---

### Step 3 — Core Database (Weeks 2–4) — “80/20 words”

**Goal:** build a small word set that powers many sentences.

Target: **~625 high‑frequency items** (words + chunks).

#### What to learn first (groups)

* **Connectors:** and, but, because, if, so, although
* **Core verbs:** be, have, do, go, get, make, take, give, want, need
* **Pronouns:** I, you, he, she, it, we, they
* **Time words:** now, today, tomorrow, always, never, usually
* **Question tools:** what, where, when, why, how
* **Polite tools:** please, sorry, excuse me

#### “No translation” rule (what it really means)

Goal: concept → English, not Spanish → English.

* Bad card: “dog = perro”.
* Better card: picture/audio of dog → “dog”.

If you *must* use Spanish sometimes, use it only as a temporary scaffold.

#### SRS card templates (simple)

* **Word card:** Image + audio → type or say the word.
* **Chunk card:** Audio of a phrase → repeat + type.
* **Cloze card:** “I ____ to go.” (want/need/have) → choose the right one.

**Daily actions (20–40 min):**

* New cards: 10–20/day.
* Reviews: do them every day, no skips.

**Gate (Database):**

* You can use 300+ of your items in short sentences without Spanish help.
* In SRS, your mature cards stay above a stable success rate (example: 80–90%).

---

### Step 4 — Immersion Loop (Week 4 onward) — “Sequencing”

**Goal:** turn pieces into real language.

You need both:

* **Input** (listen/read)
* **Output** (speak/write)

#### Input (30–60 min/day)

Use content where you understand **70–80%**.

* If you understand 95%, it is too easy.
* If you understand 40%, it is too hard.

**Rule:** do not stop every 5 seconds to translate.
Instead:

1. Keep going.
2. Mark the unknown word.
3. Look it up later (only the important ones).

#### Output (10–30 min/day)

Pick one:

* Talk to a tutor/partner.
* Record yourself answering prompts.
* Write short texts and get corrections.

**The “Mistake Rule”:** speak from day one.
Sound stupid fast. Fix fast.

#### “Glue grammar” (only when stuck)

Do not study grammar like a religion.
Use it like a wrench.

When you get stuck:

1. Find the smallest rule that fixes the problem.
2. Make 10 example sentences.
3. Put 3 of them into SRS.

**Gate (Immersion):**

* You can hold a 10‑minute conversation using simple words.
* You can understand a beginner podcast/video without subtitles most of the time.

---

### Step 5 — Language Islands (start in Week 4)

**Goal:** have safe starting scripts for real talk.

Build 3–4 islands:

1. Who I am
2. What I do
3. My hobbies
4. Why I am learning English

**Template (simple):**

* I am…
* I live in…
* I work/study…
* I like…
* Right now, I am learning English because…
* My next goal is…

**Gate (Islands):**

* You can say each island smoothly, with good rhythm, without long pauses.
* You can answer 5 follow‑up questions about each island.

---

### Step 6 — Stakes (Module IV) — Your domain

**Goal:** be competent under pressure.

Pick ONE domain:

* Work meetings
* Job interviews
* University lectures
* Travel + airports
* Tech + programming
* Sales + negotiation
* Any other domain you care about

**Actions:**

1. Make a “domain list” of 50 words/chunks you truly need.
2. Make 10 role‑play scenarios.
3. Practice with time pressure.

**Gate (Stakes):**

* You can complete 3 scenarios with: clear speech, correct core grammar, and good repair (“Sorry, I mean…”) when you fail.

---

## 7) The universal daily routine (simple)

Use this minimal routine when busy:

* **SRS:** 15–25 minutes (non‑negotiable)
* **Input:** 20 minutes
* **Output:** 5–10 minutes

When you have more time:

* Add pronunciation drills.
* Add longer conversations.

---

## 8) The universal gate system (how you move forward)

For each sub‑skill (a sound, a grammar pattern, a word group):

1. **Explain:** you learn it in simple words.
2. **Check:** you paraphrase it.
3. **Test:** you answer questions that require prediction and creation.
4. **Gate:** you need **3 correct answers in a row** (or 3 out of 4) to move on.

If you fail:

* You do not “restart life.”
* You go back one small step, fix the weak piece, then test again.

## 9) Spanish‑speaker pitfalls (and fixes)

These are common traps. Use them as a checklist.

### Pitfall 1 — Dropping the subject

Spanish can omit the subject. English usually cannot.

* Spanish: “Estoy cansado.”
* English: “**I** am tired.”

**Fix:** train “I / you / he / she / we / they” until it is automatic.

### Pitfall 2 — Articles (a/an/the)

English uses articles constantly.

**Fix (simple rule):**

* **a/an** = one of many
* **the** = the one we both know

### Pitfall 3 — Negation and questions need helpers

English often uses **do/does/did**.

* I eat apples.
* I **do not** eat apples.
* **Do** you eat apples?

**Fix:** add “do‑support” into your golden sentence set early.

### Pitfall 4 — False friends

Example: “actually” ≠ “actualmente.”

**Fix:** collect false friends as a special deck.

### Pitfall 5 — Phrasal verbs

“get up, turn on, figure out.”

**Fix:** learn them as **chunks**, not as separate words.

---

## 10) Progress tracking (simple)

Track only what matters:

* Minutes of SRS (done / not done)
* Minutes of input
* Minutes of output
* One “big mistake of the day” + fix

If you do this, you will not lie to yourself.

---

## 11) Mastery tests (simple but real)

Use these as “final bosses.”

### Test A — Prediction

Listen to a sentence and predict the next word or the next grammar shape.

### Test B — Creation

Given a situation, build 5 correct sentences fast.

### Test C — Repair

When you make a mistake, you can fix it in real time:

* “Sorry, I mean…”
* “Let me say that again.”

### Test D — Transfer

You can use the same pattern in a new topic (not just memorized sentences).

---

## 12) Appendix: upgraded golden set (optional)

After you finish Step 1, add 6 more sentences that expose missing English mechanics:

* Do you like apples? (question formation)
* I don’t like the apple. (do‑negation)
* I didn’t eat the apple. (past + did)
* I’m eating the apple. (present continuous)
* I’ve been eating apples. (perfect continuous)
* If I had time, I would study. (conditional)

Use the same analysis style.

---

## 13) How to use this manual with an AI tutor

If you use a tutor bot:

* Always tell it your **current step**.
* Ask it to test you with **new scenarios**, not memorized examples.
* Demand **gates** before moving on.

This is how you avoid feeling progress without having progress.
